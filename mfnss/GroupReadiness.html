How Are My Students Working Together?

A quick needs-assessment and observation guide for classroom collaboration

Purpose

Before you redesign group structures or stations, take a snapshot of how students currently work together. Use this tool to surface strengths, locate gaps, and decide which collaboration skills to teach next.

1) Quick Self-Check

Rate each statement from 1 (Never) to 5 (Consistently). Circle/mark on paper or click radios before printing.

Statement 1

Consistently
Students understand the learning goal behind their group task.
I form groups intentionally (purpose, readiness, or interests).

Students share responsibility rather than “dividing and disappearing.”
Groups complete tasks efficiently without constant teacher direction.
Students talk about ideas and reasoning, not just answers.
Students resolve small disagreements respectfully.
Each group produces evidence of joint thinking (not just combined pieces).
I observe and record patterns during group work.
I use observation/reflection data to regroup students or adjust tasks.

2) Collaboration Micro-Skills Observation Matrix

Skim a few groups for 5–10 minutes. For each category, scan the micro-skills, select a frequency, and jot a quick note or evidence.

Category Micro-skills / What to Look For Frequency Notes / Evidence
Communication & Listening
  • Active listening (eye contact, nods)
  • Paraphrasing or restating ideas
  • Asking clarifying questions
  • Building on peer comments
  • Inclusive language (“we”, “our plan”)
Roles & Participation
  • Roles clear/visible and rotated
  • Shared handling of materials/tasks
  • Peer accountability (not just teacher)
  • Balanced airtime & contributions
Collaboration Processes
(People Skills)
  • Shared decision-making; consensus seeking
  • Respectful conflict language (“I see it differently…”)
  • Inviting quieter voices
  • Recognizing group progress (micro-celebrations)
Project Management
(Task Skills)
  • Task broken into steps / checklist made
  • Timeline visible; timekeeper active
  • Responsibility chart (who does what)
  • Mid-task progress checks; plan adjusted if needed
Problem-Solving & Reasoning
  • “Why/what if” questions; considering alternatives
  • Using evidence/examples to justify choices
  • Documenting reasoning (sketch, notes, doc)
Reflection & Metacognition
  • Quick recap of what was learned
  • Exit ticket or summary card completed
  • Identify next step or question
Use of Tools & Technology
  • Shared doc/whiteboard to make thinking visible
  • Students replay directions (video/QR/LMS) independently
  • Tech/AI supports—not replaces—thinking

3) Quick Observation Activity — Elementary

Try:

Build the Tallest Paper Tower or Sequence the Story Cards (10 minutes).

Look for:

  • Who reads or restates the directions?
  • Who takes initiative first? Who invites others in?
  • How are materials and turns shared?
  • How do they decide when the task is “done”?

3) Quick Observation Activity — Secondary

Try:

Analyze a historical source or solve a multi-step math problem in pairs/groups (10–12 minutes).

Look for:

  • Are roles clear or improvised?
  • How are disagreements handled?
  • Do students explain reasoning or only share answers?
  • Who tracks time and progress?

Quick note:

4) Reflection & Next Steps

Use your notes to choose the next one or two skills to teach explicitly.

  • Which micro-skills seem strongest across groups?
  • Which are missing or inconsistent?
  • Are students stronger in people skills or project management skills?
  • What small routine or mini-lesson could build the next skill?
